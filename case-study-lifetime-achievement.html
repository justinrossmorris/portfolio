Lifetime Achievement Award Speech

A 3-minute speech for Insight CEO Joyce Mullen receiving the industry's highest honor for women in technology, delivered to 400+ women at a black-tie gala.

3 min

Speech Runtime

Featured

1 Overview

Insight CEO Joyce Mullen was selected for the industry's most prestigious lifetime achievement award for women in technology. The ceremony: a black-tie gala at Gotham Hall in New York City, with 400+ senior women leaders in attendance.

Joyce needed an acceptance speech. Not a keynote, something shorter, more personal. Three minutes to capture 30+ years of career wisdom and leave the room moved.

The stakes were high. This was her capstone moment, delivered just weeks after announcing her retirement. The speech had to honor her legacy while inspiring the next generation.

2 Challenge

Acceptance speeches are deceptively difficult:

  • •Brevity under pressure: Three minutes isn't much. Every sentence has to earn its place. No room for corporate filler.
  • •Personal without being self-congratulatory: The audience came to celebrate Joyce, but she wanted to celebrate them. The speech had to redirect the spotlight.
  • •Emotional resonance: This wasn't a business presentation. It needed to land in the heart, not just the head.
  • •A story worth telling: We needed a narrative anchor, something memorable that would carry the message long after the applause.

3 Approach

Finding the story: In preparing interview responses for the press coverage, we'd surfaced a story Joyce tells often, about being asked to fetch coffee at a meeting early in her career, only to take her seat at the table moments later as a key participant. The man who asked "wanted to disappear from embarrassment." She never said a word.

This story had everything: humor, tension, a twist, and a powerful lesson about quiet confidence. It became the spine of the speech.

Structure design:

  1. The hook: Open with the coffee story. Make them lean in.
  2. The pivot: "Here's what I've learned..." Shift from personal anecdote to universal truth.
  3. The tribute: Turn the spotlight to the audience. "This community taught me."
  4. The charge: Leave them with a call to action they can carry home.

Voice and delivery: I wrote stage directions into the script: [BREATH — TAKE IN THE ROOM], [BEAT — SLIGHT SMILE], [SLOW — SHIFT TONE]. These cues helped Joyce own the pacing and let the moments land.

4 Sample Excerpt

Opening

"Early in my career, I had a meeting I'll never forget. I walked into the conference room with a strategy I'd been working on. Before we started... one of the men at the table looked at me and asked if I'd make him a coffee. He told me exactly how he liked it: two sugars, one cream.

So... I made the coffee.

I handed it to him. And then I took MY seat at the table.

I watched that man try to disappear into his chair for the rest of the meeting."

The Pivot

"Here's what I've learned in 30 years in this industry: You don't have to win every battle with words. Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is just... take your seat. And be so good they can't ignore you."

The Close

"The next time someone underestimates you — and they will — don't waste your energy on the argument.

Make the coffee. Take your seat. Do the work. Be the woman who knows EXACTLY where she belongs."

5 Impact

The speech landed. The trade publication covering the event quoted extensively from the speech in their feature article, using it to close their coverage:

"The next time someone underestimates you, and they will, don't waste your energy on an argument. Make the coffee. Serve it with a smile. Be the person who knows exactly where you belong."

From the event coverage

The coffee story became the defining narrative of the evening. Colleagues who attended reported it was the moment people were talking about afterward.

More importantly: this was Joyce's last major public speech as CEO. The words had to be worthy of the moment. They were.

What This Demonstrates

  • ✓ Story architecture: Building a 3-minute speech around a single powerful anecdote that carries the entire message
  • ✓ Voice authenticity: Writing in Joyce's natural rhythms, the pauses, the humor, the directness
  • ✓ Emotional calibration: Knowing when to let a moment breathe and when to drive home
  • ✓ High-stakes delivery support: Stage directions that help the speaker own the room

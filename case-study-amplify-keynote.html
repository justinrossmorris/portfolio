Sales Kickoff Keynote

1 Overview

A newly hired SVP was scheduled to deliver his first keynote at the company's annual sales kickoff — the biggest internal event of the year. He'd been in the role for just 122 days.

The stakes: introduce himself to 1,500+ field sellers, establish credibility, drive enthusiasm for a new go-to-market strategy, and bring a customer on stage for a live fireside chat. All in 25 minutes.

His mantra: "Refuse to lose." The challenge: turn that energy into a speech that would make an entire salesforce feel the same way.

2 Challenge

This wasn't a typical keynote. The complexity came from multiple angles:

  • •New executive, high exposure: His first time on the company's biggest stage. The audience would be evaluating him as much as listening to him.
  • •Complex strategic messaging: The content involved technical concepts (infrastructure, AI transformation) that had to land for a sales audience without getting into the weeds.
  • •Customer fireside embedded: A CIO would join him on stage for a live Q&A. The questions had to be sharp enough to elicit real insights but flexible enough for natural conversation.
  • •High-energy delivery expectations: He's a dynamic speaker who feeds off the room. The script couldn't feel scripted — it had to give him room to move.

3 Approach

Finding the hook: In our early conversations, a theme emerged: he'd spent 25+ years at competitors and knew exactly how they operate. He wasn't just new to the company — he was a convert. That positioning became the foundation: "I've sat in the room with your competition. And I'm here to tell you: they don't have what we have."

The boxing metaphor: His personal mantra — "Refuse to lose" — suggested a competitive, fighting spirit. We built the entire talk around a boxing theme: fuchsia gloves, a Rocky-inspired entrance, and language about "getting in the ring" with customers.

Two-deck architecture: To give him maximum flexibility on stage, I built two synchronized decks:

  • •VISUALS deck (audience screen) — Clean, bold imagery. Minimal text. Let him be the show.
  • •NOTES deck (confidence monitor) — Bullet-point prompts, not full scripts. Enough to keep him on track, loose enough to let him riff.

Iterative refinement: We went through 11 versions of the storyboard. Each iteration moved further from "script" toward "prompts." In our final prep call, he ran through the entire talk without slides — just from memory, using the structure we'd built together.

Fireside prep: For the customer Q&A, I developed 7 questions that moved through a clear arc: set the scene, surface the challenge, get to the "why us" moment, and close with advice for the room. I also briefed him on what to do if the customer was a no-show (backup plan: pull a sales leader from the audience).

4 Sample Excerpt

Opening — After the Rocky Entrance

"[Takes off boxing gloves]

Alright, let me take these off before I hurt somebody.

122 days at this company. And I have never been more certain about anything in my career: nobody can do what we do.

Let me show you why."

Establishing Credibility

"I've spent twenty-five years with your competition. Sitting with them. Watching how they turn up, how they deliver, how they create value. I know how they operate.

We can beat them. But only if we stop showing up as order takers and start showing up as trusted advisors."

The Close

"122 days. 100% certain. Nobody can do what we do.

[Picks up boxing gloves]

Now let's go win. Refuse to lose."

5 The Deliverables

This project required more than a script. The full package included:

  • •Speaker brief: A comprehensive document covering logistics, stage directions, contingency plans, and guidance for handling everything from time crunches to technical failures.
  • •Storyboard: Visual-by-visual breakdown of the entire talk, including what's on screen, what's on the confidence monitor, and key talking points.
  • •Fireside Q&A guide: Seven prepared questions with context, plus notes on what to avoid (customer NDAs, competitive landmines).
  • •VISUALS deck: 72 slides for the audience screen — bold imagery, minimal text, designed for a speaker who owns the room.
  • •NOTES deck: Synchronized confidence monitor deck with bullet-point prompts and pacing cues.

6 Impact

The entrance worked. When the Rocky theme hit and he walked out in fuchsia gloves, shadow boxing across the stage, the room came alive. He had them before he said a word.

During our final prep call — two days before the event — he ran through the entire talk without slides. Just talking through the structure we'd built together. His feedback after that session:

"Star performance, my friend. Star performance."

The real test: he made the material his own. By the time he took the stage, the script had become his voice. The prompts were just guardrails — he knew where he was going and how to get there.

What This Demonstrates

  • ✓ Executive voice development: Turning a new leader's raw energy and experience into a cohesive narrative that establishes credibility
  • ✓ Multi-format delivery: Creating interlocking assets (brief, storyboard, decks, Q&A guide) that work together as a system
  • ✓ Collaborative refinement: 11 iterations, moving from script to prompts based on how the speaker actually performs
  • ✓ High-stakes event support: First major stage moment for a new executive, with live customer integration and 1,500+ audience
